Sacred Rights of Free Expression

M: John J. McCloy,\US High Commissioner and Military Governor for Germany,
spoke recently to German citizens ‘at ceremonies marking the turning over of
Radio Stutigart to German control. The text of his broadcast follows,

Ihis transfer of the operation of the

Stuttgart radio station from the
hands of the Military Government of
the United States to private German
control affords me the first opportunity
I have had since my arrival in Germany
to deliver a message to the German
people by radio.

In the undeniably long and difficult
road back to normal living and re-
spected and responsible intercourse
with other nations, the transfer of this
radio station to German control is an-
other symbolic step. It has to date been
operated in Germany by the represent-
atives of an occupation power. That
such an instrument was used ob-
jectively and for the benefit of the
German people as much as in the
interest of those who manage it does
not alter the fact that its direction
was not in the hands of Germans.

t is now, and for the first time in

many years German owners can
determine how this station shall speak.
The station represents, like the inven-
tion of the printing press, one of
the historical developments in the
dissemination of human thought, and as
such its potential for good or evil is
apparent.

Past and present uses of broad-
casting facilities have given us
frequent and vivid examples of how
such instruments can be abused. The
air has often been defiled with all
manner of distortion and calumny. By
this transfer the Military Government
of the United States in Germany has
sought to signify the adherences of
the people of the United States to the
doctrines of free and truthful inquiry
and expression by which that country
in the course of many vicissitudes has
lived and prospered.

2

ome say such doctrines cannot

flourish in Germany. I do not
believe it. The aberrations of the recent
past cannot obscure the fact that at
least for a certain period in her history
Germany pointed the way to just such
freedom.

By this step that we take today we
seek to nourish again that old tradi-
tion, a tradition which enlightened
much of Europe for too brief a period.
It is likewise significant that control
of this station does not pass to the
unlimited control of the state. It is not
an instrument of the state with its all-
powerful tendency to favor the broad-
cast with state doctrine.

his transfer is a further earnest of

the faith we have that Germans
are capable of sustaining and enjoying
the sacred rights of free expression.
The German individual cannot enjoy
the repression of his physical liberty
or his thoughts by his leaders any
more than can the individuals of other
nations, but the world has had reasons
to feel she has been far less jealous of
these rights as far as his fellowmen
were concerned than he could well
have been. Here is now a further
opportunity to demonstrate to the
world that left to their own devices,
Germans are as capable as others of
maintaining and preserving free in-
stitutions,

In transferring this station we do so
in the great hope that Radio Stuttgart
under its new management will in a
measurable period of time become
know and accepted throughout Europe
as an objective, efficient and thorough-
ly reliable disseminator of news, enter-
tainment and culture to all who listen.

West Germany Votes

A SIGNIFICANT milestone in the
revival of democratic _ self-
government in Germany will be
reached on Sunday, August 14, when
the election for the first Bundestag
(popular assembly) as the lower
chamber of the parliament of the
West German Federal Republic is
called, will be held in the US,
British and French Zones,

It will mark the first time that a
fair and democratic election on
anything approaching a_ national
scale will have taken place in Ger-
many since the Reichstag (national
assembly) election of November
1932, a little more than two months
before Adolf Hitler was appointed
chancellor and proceeded to destroy
the Weimar Republic. The refusal
of the Soviet Government to imple-
ment the Potsdam Agreement and to
give effect to the political unification
of Germany has resulted unfortu-
nately in the exclusion of the East
Zone from this poll, But even with
this drawback the parliamentary
election of August 14 will be an
important stage in the restoration of
a democratic German Republic and
will give the world for the first time
since the war a picture of how the
German people view the problem of
their national reconstruction,

Dr. Konrad Adenauer, president of the
Parliamentary Council at Bonn, heads
the Christian Democratic Party.

Nobody knows exactly today how
many people will be eligible to vote
in the Bundestag election. An estimate

zones in 1946 and 1947, The great
bulk of these new voters are ref-

possibly decisive factor in this elec-

21 years old and who have been

the time of the establishment of the
Western German Federal Government,

Upon establishment of the German
government, the US, UK and French
Military Governments will be re-
constituted as an element of a tri-
partite High 'Commission for Germany
headed by a high commissioner rep-
resenting each power.

The new organizational plan calls
for eight major operating units, a
central secretariat anda field division,
each headed by a ranking individual
who, in addition to the deputy high
commissioner, will constitute Mr.
McCloy’s “cabinet”.

The ten principal assistants will be
the directors of offices dealing with
Economic Affairs, Political Affairs,
Military Security, Labor, Intelligence,
Public Affairs, Administration, a
General ' Counsel, an Executive
Secretary and the head of a Field
division,

there was no intention to decrease

could not well be separated.

deputy military governor, to direct
the development of detailed plans

Gen. Clay Receives
1949 Freedom Award

General Lucius D, Clay, former US
Military Governor of Germany, and
David E. Lilienthal, chairman of the
US Atomic Energy Commission, are
the winners of the 1949 Freedom
Award for outstanding dedication to
public service and the ideals of
freedom,

It is the first time since the annual
award was instituted eight years ago
that a dual selection has been made.
Announcing the winners, Robert P.
Patterson, president of Freedom
House, explained the 1949 precedent-
breaking award thus:

“This dual selection is prompted by
our belief that both men have in equal
measure dedicated themselves to
public service and the ideal of
freedom, Each thas directed a vast
undertaking for which there was no
previous chart. Each has had to over-
come almost insurmountable obstacles,
thoughtless opposition, and _totali-
tarian pressures. Through the efforts
of these men, democracy has been
strengthened.”

Other Freedom Award winners have
been Senator Arthur H. Vandenberg,
former chairman of the Senate Foreign
Relations Committee; the late Wendell
Willkie, noted liberal and the Re-
publican Party's presidential candidate
in 1940; Walter Lippman, newspaper
columnist; Sumner Welles, former US
Undersecretary of State; General
Dwight D. Eisenhower, “Elder States-
man" Bernard M. Baruch, and former
US Secretary of State George C.
Marshall.

This year's plaques, to be presented
October 13, will be inscribed “To
General Lucius D, Clay, under whose
resolute command democracy spread
its wings to lift our hopes for peace,”
and “To David E. Lilienthal, path-
finder of a new age, harnessing nature
and science in the service of democ-
racy."

ed persons to decide on election day

out by the Bundestag.

Modern German Art

ERLIN, in spite of its unhappy re-
cent years of economic and poli-
tical troubles, is now experiencing the
most vigorous activity in the field of
fine arts of any city in Germany.

Many members of the American
community, along with Allied art
lovers, are not merely interested in
looking at contemporary Berlin art
but are serving as art patrons or are
doing a bit of painting themselves.
Moreover, they are, by virtue of their
being in the four power capital along
with nationals. of other countries,
helping to provide that international
cosmopolitan atmosphere in which the
fine arts best grow and prosper.

In this great metropolis, despite the
shortages of paint, canvas, fuel, food,
electricity and the many amenities
found in modern cities, the artists

have an uninhibited vitality and a
burning desire to work ceaselessly to
express vigorous free philosophies in
strong forms and new colors.

IHE MEASUREMENT of the cost
of living has been of concern to
Allied as well as German officials
of the Bizonal Area since the be-
ginning of the occupation. The
changes in living costs have been of
great public interest since monetary
reform. From the point of view of
workers and employees, these chan-
ges represented changes in real in-
come by which they ware vitally
affected. For businessmen the cost-
of- living index was one of the few
available measures of the state of
the market, and for government
officials it indicated, in a sense, the
success or failure of monetary reform.
There have, therefore, been con-
tinual efforts toward setting up and
improving cost-of-living indexes for
the US and Britsh Zones as well as
for each of the states. The latest
effort culminating in a new index
takes account of the transformation
in the economy of the Bizonal Area
resulting from monetary reform and
related actions, It is called a “con-
sumer price index” rather than a
“cost-of-living index“ because the
new index measures changes in
prices paid by consumers for a fixed
quantity of goods.

osophy which draws a sharp line

the source of ideas.

Postal Progress

A vast improvement in the quality
of postal service by the Deutsche
Post in the Bizonal Area was noted
during the past year, According to a
recent survey by the Deutsche Post
of over 30,000 pieces of mail, no air-
mail from any part of the world takes
longer than eight days for delivery
to bizonal addresses.

All airmail from North America
reaches addresses within seven days,
and 72 percent within four days. A
year ago only 40 percent of airmail
from North America reached its bi-
zonal destination within four days,
while some took as long as 20 days.

It takes 15 days for most inter-
national surface mail to be delivered,
and no more than twice that time for
the slowest deliveries. While the
extremes in elapsed time for surface
mail are about the same now as in
1948, the bulk of the volume is now
delivered from 5-10 days earlier.

A year ago parcel post service
from the United States took as much
as 70 days for delivery, but currently
none takes more than 50, and more
than half is delivered within 30 days.

The greatest factor in the improved
service is the speedier handling of
mail matter within Germany, the
survey indicates.

Nationalism and the Election

The outcome of the August 14 elec-
tion of the first federal diet of the
Federal Republic of Germany “will go
far to determine both German progress
toward a workable democracy and the

“Overshadowed by more dramatic
world events, an election campaign is
getting under way in Germany which
is bound «to have repercussions far
beyond. that country and poses: many
delicate problems for both the Germans
and the Western Powers. This is. the
campaign for the election of the first
federal diet of the Federal Republic of
Germany, ‘scheduled to take place
August 14

“It will be the first free national
election in Germany since the advent
of Hitler, and its outcome will go far
to determine both German progress
toward a workable democracy and the
stability of the new German state
established under western auspices.

“In that respect, the new election
law, which is weighted in favor of
direct election by majority vote, holds
out better promise than the previous
system of straight proportional repre-
sentation which wrecked the Weimar
Republic and proved to be the Trojan
horse for totalitarianism, although the
effects of a mixture of both these
systems embodied in the law remains
to be seen.

strains which will require considerable
statesmanship to overcome. For the
election is taking place in an occupied
country in which the supreme authority
remains in the hands of the occupying
powers.

“These powers are pledged to create
a peaceful, free and prosperous Ger-
many capable of being integrated into
the European family of nations, for
which purpose they are extending to
the Western Germans both economic
aid and’ protection against the menace
of being absorbed by the Soviets. In

Coal Industry Reorganization

The general director of the Deutsche Koh-
lenbergbau-Leitung (German Coal Administra-
tion) and the President of the Industriegewerk-
schaft Bergbau (German Mining Industry
Union) have been informed that the US/UK
Military Governments have decided to entrust
to the Deutsche Kohlenbergbau-Leitung in
consultation with its Beirat (Advisory Com-
mittee) the task of developing and preparing
plans for the reorganization of the coal indus-
try under the provision and within the scope
of US and UK Military Government Laws
No. 75.

The DKBL therefore has been instructed by
the Combined Coal Control Group to
commence immediately the preparation. of
plans for the reorganization of the Coal Indu-
siry under Law No. 75. It has been made
clear to the DKBL that the plan submitted
should represent the conclusion of DKBL
reached indepedently of any previous dis-
cusssions or interpretations concerning Law
No. 75,

The DKBL was instructed ‘very definitely
fully to coordinate the works of these plans
with the Advisory Committee, and that due
consideration must be given to the views of,
that body. This is very important, since the
Beirat consists of equal representation of
management and the trade union. The DKBL
was also instructed to maintain close touch
with the Steel Trustee Association in order
that the organization plan‘ of the coal, iron
and steel industries might be properly co-
ordinated.

Because of the Military Government
decision with respect to the reorganization
proposal, the Combined Coal Control Group
indicated that it would no longer require the
services of the VITS Committee which the
group had appointed to advise on financial
and legal problems arising on the implementa-
tion of Law No, 75, and therefore the DKBL.
was informed that, should the services and
experiences of this committee or its members
be required by them, this could be arranged
by mutual agreement between the DKBL and
the members of the committee.

It was clearly indicated that the DKBL was
free to appoint any special committee which
it may deem necessary. The plans proposed
by the DKBL after full consultation with the
Beirat for the reorganization of the coal
industry, including the financial and legal
aspects thereof, will be submitted as soon
as practicable to the Combined Coal Control
Group for approval of or modification by
Military Government,

Military Government has entrusted to the
DKBL, on which there is representation of
the Trade Union, the vital task of submitting
plans fon the reorganization of the coal indus-
try and has gone a long way to meet the
expressed wishes of the Deutsche Gewerk-
schaftsbund and the Industriegewerkschaft
Bergbau in requiring full consultation with
the Beirat of DKBL. There is now no reason
why this important and necessary reorganiza-
tion should not proceed quickly and form a
sound economic basis for the future of the
coal industry of Western Germany. — From
joint US/UK announcement.

Obstetrical Services

Although a new European Command direc-
tive authorizes the cancelation of obstetrical
services in four US Army hospitals in Ger-
many, such services will not be discontinued
at the present time in any army hospital in
the US occupation zone.

Regensburg, and the 57 Field Hospital in
Wuerzburg ‘to discontinue obstetrical services

infants will be given proper care. — From

Scrap Steel Prices

Effective July 16, 1949 price controls are
lifted for the sale of steel scrap for export
from the Bizone. The export price of cast
iron scrap, however, is reduced to $29.00
per long ton, f. a. s, North German ports or
f. 0. r, German frontier.

Previous price for steel scrap, established
by an agreement between the US and UK
Military Governors, was $ 28.50 per ton.
Under the terms of this agremeent, concluded
on 30 September, 1948, scrap prices may be
revised or suspended at any time by action
of the Military Governors. In a memorandum
published July 13 the US and UK Economic
Advisors stated that the new provisions
would apply until further notice and that
prices for steel scrap would be according to
agreements between the contracting parties.

The lifting of price controls will not affect
the terms of existing scarp contracts, unless
the contracts contain provisions for readjust-
ment or renegotiation of prices.

A previous regulation, requiring screening
of buyers by JEIA, was cancelled on July 1,
1949. As of that date, sales of scrap were
made in accordance with the revised JEIA
Instruction No.1, the basic regulation for
commercial exports. At the same time, JEIA
cancelled its regulation requiring scrap: buy-
ers to deposit ‘earnest money’ which ranged
from 10 to 20 per cent of the contract value.
Final approval of all scrap contracts is still
required by the main office of JEIA. — From
JEIA announcement.

Shipping Restrictions Lifted

All geographical limitations on German
shipping have been lifted effective July 1.

The new policy was effected through the
cancellation of paragraph 5 of JEIA Instruction
No. 17, dealing with management of German
shipping. This paragraph limited trading by
German ships to Brest, the United Kingdom,
Scandinavia and Finland.

Paragraph 5 also restricted German ships to
the carriage of German exports and German
imports or transit cargoes for German ports,
with certain exceptions. With the cancellation
of this paragraph, these restrictions are also
eliminated.

These changes are expected to permit the
inauguration of liner freight services to Spain
and Mediterranean, Adriatic, North African
and Near East ports. A number of applications
for such services are now awaiting approval.

Until now, the bulk of German shipping
activities has been tramp trading in the Baltic
and North Sea, though about a dozen cargo
liner services in these areas have been
operating.

Western Germany's fleet now consists of
about 1,100 ships, biqger of about 270,000 tons.
—From JEIA announcement.

Many Germans feel that their present
school system compares favorably
with that of other countries. Just why
does Military Government believe
that a school reform is necessary?

German secondary schools have
long been recognized as_ scholas-
tically sound, but their training serves
a very small segment of the total
population. A secondary education
for all is essential to intelligent
citizenship, which in turn is essential
to a democracy.

In the United States, for example,
about 50 percent of the total school
population is graduated from secon-
dary schools. Here in Germany only
about 10 percent complete a secon-
dary school education. In other words,
in Germany too few pupils are get-
ing a secondary education, Represen-

tative government needs a much
higher percentage of people trained
in citizenship than is now the case in
Germany. (OMGWB)
