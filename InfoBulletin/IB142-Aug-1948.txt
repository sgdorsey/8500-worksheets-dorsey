VERY EUROPEAN country today,

with the possible exception of
Switzerland, is suffering under an
austerity program very similar to that
of Germany. These countries can only
afford to buy as much as they can sell;
or, to put it in another way, the more
each country sells, the more it can
afford to buy.

Hence the tremendous importance
of the European Recovery Program,
which is already pumping dollars into
the economies of Europe, dollars which
will flow from one national treasury
into another, expediting and lubricat-
ing the exchange of products, manu-
factured and unprocessed, to the
mutual advantage of all. Thus, for
example, the Italians have been able
to sell surplus fruits and vegetables
to Bizonal Germany, using the dollar
proceeds for purchases in Germany.

Why is it necessary to have agree-
ments at all? Why cannot German
businessmen simply carry on trade
with foreign buyers or foreign sellers
without recourse to painstaking nego-
tiations between the governments in-

There are two. overriding reasons
for trade agreements. Most of the
countries of Europe maintain a strictly
enforced system of import licences. As
many German exporters have learned,
perhaps to their sorrow, after signing
contracts, the latter have been subject
to cancellations because of the great
dollar shortage which exists in many
of those countries. In the second place,
commodities which are in short supply
are very rigidly controlled through
allocation to purchaser nations on a
quid pro quo basis and only through
negotiations on a government-to-
government basis can materials be
procured.

Labor Is Cautioned
On Wage Demands

The Executive Committee of the
German Bizonal Economic Council
was informed in May of the Military
Governors’ decision that measures
may now be initiated toward the
restoration of a system of collective
bargaining, under which the trade
unions and the employers would
jointly assume their proper respons-
ibility for determining wage rates.

It was pointed out that an increase
in wages would not benefit workers
on a long-term basis unless there
were a corresponding increase in the
availability of purchasable goods;
that the present situation would have
to be remedied by stages, in which
currency reform, price reform, and
an increase in the supply of consumer
goods were essential factors; and
that, as a sudden and substantial rise
in wages would only produce in-
flation, the aggregate of the increase
was not to exceed 15 percent of the
total wage bill of the persons covered
by any particular agreement.

Furthermore, it was pointed out to
the Executive Committee that a wage
increase so granted should not be
passed on to the consumers by way
of increased prices and that requests
for price increases should be sanction-
ed only in those cases where it was
clear that current prices could not
bear the additional labor costs. —

New Plan Cuts Housing Costs

Rubble, Gravel, Cement Poured into Frames

The mixture for a two-room office structure has been poured.
Metal screen frames are still attached to the new building.

WIESBADEN building firm has

perfected a construction method,
using rubble, gravel and cement,
which cuts basic house building costs
by 50 percent and construction time
by two thirds. The idea, which in-
voves pouring the rubble mixture
between removable steel screened
frames, was developed by Robert
Lusser and Otto Butter, former avi-
ation inventors.

First step in building one of the
houses is the setting up of the screened
frames, held together with long bolts,
around a pattern of water pipes and
electrical cables. Wooden window
frames are included in the dummy
structure,

The building mixture is poured and
allowed to dry, after which the steel
framing. is removed. Later, cement is
plastered over the porous compound,
into which nails can be easily driven.
The entire operation, in the case of
a four-room house, takes three days.

Company representatives said the
Bizonal Economics Administration has
placed orders for 45 houses, each
containing a cellar, living room,

Disease Rate Drops

Throughout the US Zone the Ger-
man civilian venereal disease rates
have steadily decreased. The manda-
tory control measures provided for in
Title 6 of MG Regulations have been
rigidly applied. Pressure is being
brought by German physicians for
permission to treat new cases pri-
vately with penicillin, But German
authorities have been informed that
the present directive will not be
rescinded until the passage by each
state of a satisfactory venereal disease
law modeled after Control Council Di-
rective No. 52.

Policy Clarified
On Czech Refugees

In clarifying reports previously
issued regarding supplemental feeding
of Czechoslovak refugees in the US
Zone, Raymond Ioanes, chief of the
Food and Agriculture Group, OMGUS,
explained that incoming Czechoslovak
refugees, housed in special refugee
camps, are receiving the same food
rations as the German non-farm popu-
lation.

These supplies are being furnished
from the same sources as for the Ger-
man population, namely, German in-
digenous production and supplies im-
ported by the US/UK Military Govern-
ments.

However, it was explained that re-
cently an agreement was concluded
between OMGUS and the International
Refugee Organization to provide sup-
plementary food rations of approxi-
mately 600 calories daily to each in
Czechoslovak refugee camps.

The food supplies for the supple-
ments will be furnished initially from
stocks available in the German eco-
nomy. However, replacement in kind
will be made to the German economy
by the IRO. Thus the special supple-
ments will not be at the expense of
the German population.

54 Art Treasures
Restored to Germany

Fifty-four German art treasures,
valued at more than $30,000,000, were
returned to Germany in May. These
constituted the first return shipment
from a collection of 200 paintings,
owned by the former Prussian State
museums in Berlin, which were found
in a German repository shortly after
the war and sent to the United States
for safekeeping.

The returned paintings were ex-

van Eyck, and “Flemish Folk Say-
ings” by Peter Brueghel the Elder.

Raphael, Botticelli, Rembrandt, Rubens,
Duerer and Hans Holbein the Younger.

Before being returned to Germany,
the remainder of the 200 paintings
are to be shown in several US
museums, under an arrangement
whereby the admission fees and con-
tributions earned by the exhibitions
will be used for the benefit of German
children in the US Zone. — Military

Policy on Unions
Reaffirmed by MG

Following a recent resolution of the
World Federation of Trade Unions
(WFTU) calling for the establishment
of a liaison bureau in Germany, US
Military Government reaffirmed pre-
vious declarations on the formation of
Germany-wide unions.

Germany-wide trade union movement

steps have been taken to give German
trade unions freedom in that direction,
US Military Government also expres-

quisites would soon be realized.

These conditions are: (1) agreement
on a common definition of “demo-
cracy” and its practical implementation
by the Control Council; (2) freedom
of communication and movement
throughout Germany; (3) all-German
economic unity, and (4) genuine politi-
cal freedom in all parts of Germany.

Concerning the WFTU desire for a
liaison office, US Military Government
declared that it could not accord the
WFTU any rights not simultaneously
extended to other bona fide and re-
sponsible union organizations through-
out Germany.— Military Governor's

the labor ministers to approve directly

Military Government, with written cer-

the raises do not exceed the authori-

manner of calculating each increase.

After the new wage policy was
announced, considerable resistance
developed among private and public
enterprises to the negotiation of new
wage agreements,

negotiation of agreements should be
postponed until after the expected cur-
rency reform, when their financial
position would become clearer. Public
enterprises argued that granting in-
creases would absorb all of their sur-
plus revenue, with the result that they
would be unable to contribute to the
revenue of the Bizonal Area. —

CONTROL OF GERMAN THEATERS
The director of Information Control Divi-
sion, OMGUS, is charged with the control of
information services for the German civilian
population in the US Zone of occupation.
In order to expedite cooperation with the
Information Control Division in carrying out
its mission:

Where movie houses, theaters or concert
halls are now used for troop entertainment,
these principles will prevail:

1, In each locality where such facilities exist
in usable condition, at least one movie house
will be set aside for German civilian use.
In large cities, several movie houses of
suitable location and capacity will be made
available for German civilian use on a full-
time basis. In each locality where such facili-
ties exist in usable condition, at least one
theater, one opera house and one concert
hall will be made available part-time or full-
time for German civilian use. In localities
where only one such facility exists in usable
condition, a sharing of time between troops
and German civilians will be arranged.

2. Under conditions where audiences can be
admitted and dismissed at advertised hours,
part-time use of theaters and halls for troops
and part-time for German civilians is autho-
rized. This will apply generally only to movie
exhibitions and orchestral or musical concerts.
Full-time occupancy of theaters where operas
and plays are presented is desirable wherever
possible.

3. Performances organized exclusively for
troops will mot be given under the auspices
of impresarios, managers, intendants or pro-
moters who have not previously been licensed
by District Information Control Division. Per-
formances organized exdlusively for troops
will not be given German performers who
are on the black list of the Information Control
Division, OMGUS.

4, Military organizations will not requisition
costumes, scenery, properties, musical instru-
ments, or other equipment required for use
in presenting operas, plays or concerts,
without coordinating with District Information
Control Division.

It is the policy of this headquarters that
troops will not be billeted in movie houses,
theaters or concert halls which are in suffi-
ciently good repair to be used for perfor-
mances, — From Eucom Cir 47.

Instructions

No technical experience is necessary to
detect a counterfeit. Merely look at or study
a genuine certificate occasionally to become
more familiar with its appearance, One out-
standing irregularity that can be recognized
on a certificate will justify its classification
as counterfeit and its detention for further
examination by either the Finance Office,
Provost Marshal, or the Criminal Investigation
Division.

Following are outstanding defects and imper-
fections which are readily visible to the naked
eye which appear on counterfeit $5 and
$10 certificates recently discovered in the
zone. Good military payment certificates look
good, while bad military payment certificates
look bad.

Counterfeit $5 certificates (Series 472). Eagle
on face of the certificate:

The blue ink on the wings runs into solid
color and shows no refined engraving lines.
The edges of the wings are blunt stubs,
abruptly terminated, with no feather edges,

The arrows clutcied by the eagle's left
claw show solid blue. The arrows here are
indistinct and crudely executed. No berries
are visible on the branch clutched by the
eagle's right claw. 5

Base of the eagle's tail is solid blue an
tail feathers are poorly executed,

The vertical blue stripes on the eagle's
breast shield are a solid blue. The genuine
certificate shows three separate blue lines.

The fishnet design in the border is broken
by irregular lines, leaving white blotches and
showing am ashen gray color.

The eagle on the back of the military
payment certificate is crudely executed with
poor engraving and blotchy printing. The
vertical stripes of the breast shield on the
eagle are solid brown ‘with no rulings. Defects
are generally similar to those on the face
of the certificates.

RADIOSONDE APPARATUS

Concerning inquiries from German civilians
and other sources requesting disposition in-
structions regarding recovered radiosonde
apparatus, the following disposition instruc-
tions are furnished:

US Army radiosonde equipment is expend-
able and when recovered from open fields
or similar areas is of no further value to
the US Army or Air Force, and therefore
should be destroyed.

Any recovered radiosonde equipment bearing
markings of either French manufacture 0”
French identification should be held and
reported to the French National Weather
Office, Paris, France, by the agency receiving
or coming into possession of the item. —
From Sec IX, EUCOM Weekly Directive No. 27.
